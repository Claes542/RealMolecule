\begin{abstract}
We observe that a single Coulomb / multiphase-continuum model (RealQM) describes \emph{both} atomic and
nuclear binding through a \textbf{variable-size electron}, using only three inputs --- the charge / Coulomb
potential $e^2$, an electron kinetic coefficient $\kappa$, and the proton's finite size $R_p$ --- with
\emph{no mass and no strong force}. The electron has no fixed size: it adapts to its environment, spreading
to the \textbf{atomic} scale $a_0 = 2\kappa/e^2$ when bound to a single proton's charge, and compacting to
the \textbf{nuclear} scale, set by the proton-size cage, where a flat caged density on a multiphase free
boundary carries \emph{zero kinetic energy}. The nuclear/atomic size ratio is then the fine-structure
constant squared,
\[ \alpha^2 = \frac{R_p}{a_0} = \frac{\text{proton size}}{\text{atomic size}}, \]
so $\alpha$ is reinterpreted \textbf{geometrically} --- the proton-to-atom size ratio, not a coupling
constant, and requiring no $c$. The measured deuteron/hydrogen size ratio matches $1/\alpha^2$ to within
${\sim}30\%$ (the deuteron sitting at the classical electron radius). Mass appears nowhere in this
electrostatic structure; it is a separate, inertial/gravitational quantity (the matter-wave's inverse
size). Finally, since the proton is rigid (fixed size) while the electron is fluid (variable size),
$\alpha$ measures the electron's \emph{dynamic range} across scales --- and the smallness of $\alpha$ is
what permits the nested, multi-scale structure of matter.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Status.} A structural/interpretive proposal within the RealQM program. The claim is the
\emph{relation} $\alpha^2=R_p/a_0$ and its consequences; the underlying primitive (the proton's finite
size, or equivalently the classical radius) and the numerical realisation are stated as open
(Section~\ref{sec:open}). A speculative cosmological extension is noted only briefly
(Section~\ref{sec:outlook}).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} fine-structure constant; atomic and nuclear scales; Coulomb binding;
multiphase continuum quantum mechanics; classical electron radius; proton radius; RealQM.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Why is the nucleus roughly $10^4$--$10^5$ times smaller than the atom that contains it? In standard physics
the atomic scale is electromagnetic (set by $\hbar,m,e$) and the nuclear scale is set by the strong force ---
two unrelated sectors --- and the fine-structure constant $\alpha=e^2/\hbar c$ enters as the coupling of
the electromagnetic interaction. Here we point out that within the RealQM formulation of quantum mechanics
as multiphase Coulomb continuum mechanics~\cite{realqm,realnucleus} --- unit-charge densities on
non-overlapping domains separated by Bernoulli free boundaries, minimising the Coulomb energy --- both the
atomic and the nuclear scale arise from \emph{one} mechanism with a \emph{variable-size} electron, and
$\alpha$ acquires a direct geometric meaning: the ratio of the proton size to the atomic size. The argument
is elementary and uses no relativity; we keep its open points explicit.

\section{The model and its three inputs}\label{sec:model}
An electron is a normalised charge cloud $\psi$ ($\int\psi^2\,dV=1$) of characteristic size $L$, with energy
\[ E = \underbrace{\kappa\!\int|\grad\psi|^2\,dV}_{\text{kinetic}} \;-\; \underbrace{Z\,\frac{e^2}{L}}_{\text{Coulomb}},
\qquad \kappa=\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}. \]
The whole electrostatic structure of atom and nucleus needs just three inputs:
\textbf{(i)} the charge / Coulomb potential $e^2$, \textbf{(ii)} the kinetic coefficient $\kappa$, and
\textbf{(iii)} the proton's finite size $R_p$. \emph{Mass does not appear} (it is replaced by the kinetic
coefficient $\kappa$); neither does the strong force, nor $c$. Mass and gravity form a separate sector,
discussed in Section~\ref{sec:mass}.

\section{The variable-size electron: two scales}\label{sec:twosize}
The same electron takes two forms, depending on how it is bound.

\paragraph{Outside (atomic).} Bound to a single proton, the electron must \emph{taper to zero} in the
surrounding vacuum. A varying profile carries kinetic energy ${\sim}\kappa/L^2$, and minimising
$\kappa/L^2 - e^2/L$ gives
\[ L = a_0 = \frac{2\kappa}{e^2} \quad\Big(=\frac{\hbar^2}{me^2}\Big), \]
the atomic (Bohr) scale --- set by the proton's \emph{charge}. To the atomic electron the proton's finite
size is irrelevant (it is $10^4$--$10^5$ times smaller, effectively a point).

\paragraph{Inside (nuclear).} Caged between/among protons, the electron sits on its own domain with a
multiphase \emph{free boundary} to the proton domains: its density \emph{does not drop to zero} but hands
off to the proton density. A \emph{flat} caged density then has $\grad\psi=0$ throughout, hence
\textbf{zero kinetic energy} --- there is no kinetic penalty for being small. (The familiar obstacle
``confinement to size $L$ costs $\kappa/L^2$'' applies to a \emph{vacuum} electron forced to vanish at its
edge, not to a caged one whose density stays finite at the free boundary.) With no kinetic pressure to
spread it, the electron's size is then set by the \emph{proton's finite size} --- the cage floor that stops
collapse --- and its binding is Coulomb at that size, ${\sim}e^2/R_p\sim$ MeV at fm.

So the electron is \textbf{fluid} (it adapts: $a_0$ outside, $R_p$ inside) while the proton is \textbf{rigid}
(fixed size). The crucial factor distinguishing the two scales is \emph{proton charge vs.\ proton size}.

\section{$\alpha$ as a size ratio}\label{sec:alpha}
Combining the two scales, the nuclear/atomic size ratio is
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\ \alpha^2 = \frac{R_p}{a_0} = \frac{\text{proton (nuclear) size}}{\text{atomic size}},
\qquad \alpha=\sqrt{R_p/a_0}.\ }
\end{equation}
That is, $\alpha$ is \textbf{the ratio of the proton size to the atomic size} --- a geometric quantity, not
a coupling constant, and with \emph{no $c$}. It coincides with the textbook $\alpha=e^2/\hbar c$ precisely
when $R_p$ equals the classical radius $r_e=e^2/mc^2=\alpha^2 a_0\approx 2.8$ fm. The electron's three
characteristic lengths form the ladder
\[ a_0 : \bar\lambda_C : r_e \;=\; 1 : \alpha : \alpha^2, \qquad
\bar\lambda_C=\hbar/mc \ \text{(Compton)}, \ \ r_e=e^2/mc^2 \ \text{(classical)}, \]
the three ways to build a length from $(\hbar,m,c,e)$, each a factor $\alpha$ apart. The model thus reframes
``why $\alpha\approx 1/137$'' as ``\emph{why is the proton ${\sim}$fm while the atom is ${\sim}$\AA}'' ---
relocating the question to the proton size (an input), and interpreting $\alpha$ itself as the
proton-to-atom size ratio.

\section{Empirical check}\label{sec:check}
The relation is directly testable against measured sizes:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
 & size & in fm \\
\midrule
H atom & Bohr radius $a_0=0.53$ \AA & ${\approx}\,53{,}000$ \\
deuteron nucleus & rms radius ${\approx}2.1$ fm & ${\approx}\,2.1$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The ratio is $\text{deuteron}/\text{H}\approx 4\times10^{-5}\approx 1/25{,}000$, to be compared with
$\alpha^2\approx 5\times10^{-5}\approx 1/18{,}800$ --- \textbf{agreement to ${\sim}30\%$}, with the deuteron
sitting essentially at the classical electron radius ($r_e=2.8$ fm). So the nuclear/atomic size ratio comes
out as $1/\alpha^2$ from charge, $\hbar$ and the proton size alone, with no strong force. (The deuteron is
unusually loosely bound, so this is the \emph{scale/ratio} being right, not a precise fit.)

\section{Mass is a separate quantity}\label{sec:mass}
Nothing above used mass: the electrostatic atom and nucleus are built from charge and $\kappa$. Mass is
\textbf{inertia} --- the localisation (frequency / inverse size) of the matter wave, the dispersion gap
$\omega_0=mc^2/\hbar$, equivalently $m=\hbar/c\,\bar\lambda_C^{-1}$. It is a \emph{separate, gravitational}
quantity (signed, hence not the positive-definite electromagnetic field energy), and it couples to the
electrostatic sector only through energy, $E=mc^2$. Electromagnetism (charge) is an interaction; mass is
the inverse size of each matter wave --- which is why ``the electromagnetic atom has no room for mass.''

\section{Complexity from the size asymmetry}\label{sec:complexity}
The structure-building asymmetry between proton and electron is not one of \emph{charge} (charge is exactly
balanced, $\pm e$) but of \emph{size}: the proton is fixed-and-small (a rigid anchor), the electron is
variable. The fixed proton supplies anchors; the variable electron \emph{spans scales} --- spreading to
bind atoms and molecules, compacting to bind nuclei --- producing the nested, multi-scale hierarchy that
\emph{is} complexity. With a fixed size for both, only one scale exists and nothing complex is built. And
$\alpha$ measures exactly this asymmetry: $\alpha^2=R_p/a_0$ is the electron's \emph{inverse dynamic range},
a factor $1/\alpha^2\approx18{,}800$ between its nuclear and atomic phases. \textbf{The smallness of
$\alpha$ is the engine of complexity}; equal fixed sizes would build nothing.

\section{Open problems}\label{sec:open}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The nuclear primitive.} The relation $\alpha^2=R_p/a_0$ takes the proton's finite size $R_p$
as the input nuclear length. Equivalently one may take the relativistic classical radius $r_e=\alpha^2 a_0$
(Coulomb self-energy $=$ rest energy, in the sense of Many-Minds Relativity~\cite{mmr}). Which primitive is
fundamental --- proton size or relativistic --- is open. Either way the value of $\alpha$ is \emph{not}
derived, only reinterpreted as a ratio.
\item \textbf{Numerical realisation.} Whether the multiphase solver realises a flat, zero-kinetic-energy
caged electron at the proton scale has not been cleanly confirmed; direct relaxation runs were inconclusive
(non-convergence at small cage size), and a symmetric proton cage with a stable flat density is the proper
test.
\item \textbf{Equal vs.\ unequal sizes/coefficients.} The picture is cleanest with one fixed proton size
and a variable electron; reconciling this with the measured proton/electron mass ratio (1836) is left to
the broader program.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Speculative outlook}\label{sec:outlook}
The same ingredients extend, far more speculatively, to a cosmological narrative --- equal-mass $\pm$
charges read off as the curvature of two fluctuating potentials, a gravitational collapse--bounce with
negative-mass regions acting as dark energy, and a primordial helium fraction $Y\approx0.245$ from a
compact/free-electron freeze-out. That extension is developed, with its (substantial) open problems stated
up front, in a companion essay~\cite{realcos}; it is mentioned here only to indicate scope and is not part
of the present, structural claim.
