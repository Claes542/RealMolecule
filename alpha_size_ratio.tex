\begin{abstract}
The hydrogen atom is a proton with an electron shell of size $a_0$ (the Bohr radius). The deuteron --- the
$^2$H nucleus --- is, in a pure proton--electron picture (neutron $=$ proton $+$ electron), the system
\textbf{p\,+\,e\,+\,p}: two protons bound by one shared electron, of size $R_p$ (a few fm). The two are the
\emph{same} Coulomb physics --- protons and one electron --- differing only in the electron's \textbf{size}:
bound by a single proton's \emph{charge} it relaxes to $a_0$; caged between two protons it compacts to the
proton \emph{size} $R_p$. The nuclear/atomic size ratio is then the fine-structure constant squared,
\[ \alpha^2 = \frac{R_p}{a_0} = \frac{\text{proton (deuteron) size}}{\text{atomic size}}, \]
so $\alpha$ is the \textbf{proton-to-atom size ratio} --- a pure ratio of two lengths, \textbf{independent of
the mass $m$ and of the speed of light $c$}, not a coupling constant. The relation is bracketed by two
independent quantitative validations of the same program: atomic total
energies $<1\%$ across the periodic table ($a_0$ side), and the He-4/deuteron binding-energy ratio $13.1$
vs.\ $12.7$ ($\sim3\%$, $R_p$ side). Mass plays no role in this electrostatic structure; it is a separate,
inertial/gravitational quantity. Built from charge and a kinetic coefficient alone, no strong force.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Status.} A structural/interpretive proposal within the RealQM/RealNucleus program. The claim
is the relation $\alpha^2=R_p/a_0$ read off the H atom and the deuteron; the underlying primitive (the
proton's finite size, or the classical radius) is stated as open (Section~\ref{sec:open}). A cosmological
extension is noted only briefly (Section~\ref{sec:outlook}).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} fine-structure constant; hydrogen atom; deuteron; Coulomb binding; classical
electron radius; proton radius; RealQM; RealNucleus.
\end{abstract}

\section{The question}
Why is the nucleus roughly $10^4$--$10^5$ times smaller than the atom that contains it? In standard physics
the atomic scale is electromagnetic (set by $\hbar,m,e$) and the nuclear scale is set by the strong force ---
two unrelated sectors --- and $\alpha=e^2/\hbar c$ enters as the coupling of the electromagnetic interaction.
We point out that the simplest atom and the simplest composite nucleus --- the \textbf{hydrogen atom} and the
\textbf{deuteron} --- are, in a pure proton--electron (Coulomb) picture, the \emph{same} system at two sizes,
and that their size ratio \emph{is} $\alpha^2$. The argument is elementary and uses no relativity.

\section{Two systems, one physics: the H atom and the deuteron}\label{sec:two}
An electron is a normalised charge cloud $\psi$ ($\int\psi^2=1$) with energy
$E=\kappa\!\int|\grad\psi|^2 - Z\,e^2/L$, where $\kappa=\hbar^2/2m$ is a kinetic coefficient. The whole
structure below needs only three inputs: the charge $e^2$, the coefficient $\kappa$, and the proton's finite
size $R_p$ --- \emph{no mass, no strong force, no $c$}.

\paragraph{Hydrogen atom: p\,+\,e, size $a_0$.} A single proton with one electron. Attracted to the proton's
charge, the electron must \emph{taper to zero} in the surrounding vacuum; that varying profile costs kinetic
energy ${\sim}\kappa/L^2$, and minimising $\kappa/L^2 - e^2/L$ gives
\[ L=a_0=\frac{2\kappa}{e^2}=\frac{\hbar^2}{me^2}\approx 0.53\ \text{\AA}, \]
the Bohr radius --- the electron shell size \emph{set by the proton's charge}. To this electron the proton's
finite size is irrelevant (it is $10^4$--$10^5$ times smaller, effectively a point).

\paragraph{Deuteron: p\,+\,e\,+\,p, size $R_p$.} The deuteron is the $^2$H nucleus, a proton plus a neutron.
In the pure proton--electron model (neutron $=$ p $+$ e~\cite{realnucleus}) the deuteron is therefore
\[ \text{d} = \text{p} + \text{n} = \text{p} + (\text{p}+\text{e}) = \textbf{p+e+p}, \]
two protons bound by one shared electron --- the same constituents as the H atom, plus a second proton; in
fact the nuclear analogue of the molecular ion H$_2^+$. Now the electron is \emph{caged} between the protons
on its own domain with a multiphase \emph{free boundary}: its density does \emph{not} drop to zero but hands
off to the proton density. A flat caged density then has $\grad\psi=0$, hence \emph{zero kinetic energy} ---
there is no kinetic penalty for being small. With no kinetic pressure to spread it, the electron's size is
set by the \emph{proton's finite size} $R_p$ (the cage floor that stops collapse), and its binding is Coulomb
at that size (${\sim}$MeV at fm). The electron's size is now \emph{set by the proton's size}.

\paragraph{Same physics, two sizes.} The two systems are pure proton--electron Coulomb; the electron simply
has no fixed size. The distinction is \textbf{proton charge vs.\ proton size}:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
 & constituents & electron size & binding \\
\midrule
H atom & p\,+\,e & $a_0\approx0.5$ \AA\ (charge-set) & ${\sim}13.6$ eV \\
deuteron $^2$H & p\,+\,e\,+\,p & $R_p\approx2$ fm (size-set) & ${\sim}2.2$ MeV \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\section{$\alpha$ as a size ratio}\label{sec:alpha}
Combining the two sizes, the deuteron/atom (nuclear/atomic) ratio is
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\ \alpha^2 = \frac{R_p}{a_0} = \frac{\text{deuteron (proton) size}}{\text{atomic size}},
\qquad \alpha=\sqrt{R_p/a_0}.\ }
\end{equation}
This is the textbook relation $r_e/a_0=\alpha^2$ in disguise. The electron has three lengths built from
$(\hbar,m,c,e)$ --- the Bohr radius $a_0=\hbar^2/me^2$, the reduced Compton wavelength
$\bar\lambda_C=\hbar/mc$, and the classical radius $r_e=e^2/mc^2$ --- which, using $e^2=\alpha\hbar c$, sit on
the ladder
\[ a_0:\bar\lambda_C:r_e = 1:\alpha:\alpha^2,\qquad\text{so}\qquad \frac{r_e}{a_0}=\alpha^2. \]
Our relation is this with the deuteron size identified as the classical radius, $R_p\approx r_e\approx2.8$ fm.
So $\alpha$ is reframed \textbf{geometrically} --- the proton-to-atom size ratio, not a coupling constant ---
and the question ``why $\alpha\approx1/137$'' becomes ``\emph{why is the deuteron ${\sim}$fm while the atom
is ${\sim}$\AA}'': relocated to the proton size, an input.

\paragraph{$\alpha$ is independent of $m$ and $c$.} Written as a ratio of two lengths, $\alpha=\sqrt{R_p/a_0}$
is obtained by \emph{measuring two sizes and dividing} --- the deuteron and the atom. It therefore carries
\textbf{no mass $m$ and no speed of light $c$}: it is pure geometry, dimensionless by construction, fixed once
the two lengths are given. The standard form $\alpha=e^2/\hbar c$ obscures this by writing the same number
through $c$ (and, via $a_0=\hbar^2/me^2$, through $m$); but the ratio itself needs neither. In this program
the primitive is the kinetic coefficient $\kappa$ (so $a_0=2\kappa/e^2$), not the mass, and there is no $c$ at
all --- the construction is non-relativistic and electrostatic. $\alpha$ is thus the most primitive of the
three readings: a bare geometric fact about how much larger the atom is than the nucleus. (Standard $\alpha=e^2/\hbar c$ contains $\hbar$ and
is intrinsically quantum; the only purely classical ingredient is $r_e=e^2/mc^2$, the radius at which the
electron's electrostatic self-energy equals its rest energy --- the electromagnetic-mass radius of
Lorentz--Poincar\'e~\cite{mmr}.)

\paragraph{$\alpha$ in standard quantum mechanics, for contrast.} In standard QM/QED $\alpha=e^2/\hbar c$ is
the central dimensionless constant of electromagnetism: the \emph{coupling strength} (an amplitude
$\sqrt\alpha$ at each photon vertex, so QED expands in powers of $\alpha$); the scale of the \emph{fine-structure}
splitting it is named for ($\propto\alpha^2$, a relativistic spin--orbit correction); the Bohr-orbit ratio
$v_1/c=\alpha$; and even a \emph{running} coupling that grows with energy. Every one of these meanings is tied
to $c$ and $\hbar$ --- there $\alpha$ is intrinsically relativistic and quantum. The reading here is the
opposite: the \emph{same number} as a bare \textbf{mass- and $c$-free length ratio} $\sqrt{R_p/a_0}$. The irony
is that the constant named after a relativistic spectral effect is, at root, a piece of geometry --- how much
larger the atom is than the nucleus.

\section{Empirical check}\label{sec:check}
\paragraph{The size ratio.} H atom: $a_0=0.53$ \AA\ $\approx53{,}000$ fm. Deuteron: rms radius $\approx2.1$
fm. Ratio $\text{d}/\text{H}\approx 1/25{,}000$, versus $\alpha^2\approx 1/18{,}800$ --- \textbf{agreement to
${\sim}30\%$}, the deuteron sitting essentially at the classical electron radius (2.8 fm). The nuclear/atomic
size ratio thus comes out as $1/\alpha^2$ from charge, $\hbar$ and the proton size alone, no strong force.
(The deuteron is unusually loosely bound, so this is the scale/ratio being right, not a precise fit.)

\paragraph{The two scales are independently validated, bracketing the relation.} Beyond the size ratio, the
same Coulomb program is quantitative at \emph{each} scale the relation connects:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Atomic side ($a_0$):} RealQM reproduces atomic \textbf{total energies to $<1\%$ across the whole
periodic table} (all $Z\ge11$; the period-2 p-block, B--F, the only $3$--$6\%$ exception)~\cite{atoms}.
\item \textbf{Nuclear side ($R_p$):} the same proton--electron Coulomb model gives the \textbf{He-4/deuteron
binding-energy ratio $13.1$ vs.\ $12.7$ experimental} ($\sim3\%$, nested charge-cloud shells)~\cite{realnucleus}.
\end{itemize}
So $\alpha^2=R_p/a_0$ sits between two independent quantitative validations --- the atomic scale to $<1\%$ and
the nuclear binding ratio to ${\sim}3\%$ --- not merely the ${\sim}30\%$ size check.

\section{Mass is a separate quantity}\label{sec:mass}
Nothing above used mass: the H atom and the deuteron are built from charge and $\kappa$. Mass is
\textbf{inertia} --- the localisation (frequency / inverse size) of the matter wave, the dispersion gap
$\omega_0=mc^2/\hbar$ --- a \emph{separate, gravitational} quantity (signed, hence not the positive-definite
electromagnetic field energy), coupling to the electrostatic sector only through energy, $E=mc^2$.
Electromagnetism (charge) is an interaction; mass is the inverse size of each matter wave.

\section{Complexity from the size asymmetry, and open problems}\label{sec:open}
The asymmetry between proton and electron is not one of \emph{charge} (charge is exactly balanced, $\pm e$)
but of \emph{size}: the proton is fixed-and-small (a rigid anchor), the electron variable. The fixed proton
supplies anchors; the variable electron spans scales --- a shell in the atom, a cage-filling cloud in the
deuteron --- producing the nested hierarchy that \emph{is} complexity. With a fixed size for both, only one
scale exists and nothing complex is built. And $\alpha$ measures this asymmetry: $\alpha^2=R_p/a_0$ is the
electron's inverse dynamic range ($1/\alpha^2\approx18{,}800$), so \textbf{the smallness of $\alpha$ is the
engine of complexity}.

\medskip\noindent\emph{Open.} (i) The nuclear \textbf{primitive}: the proton's finite size $R_p$ as input
length, or equivalently the relativistic classical radius $r_e=\alpha^2 a_0$ (electromagnetic mass, in the
sense of Many-Minds Relativity~\cite{mmr}); $\alpha$'s value is reinterpreted, not derived. (ii) Whether the
multiphase solver realises a flat, zero-kinetic-energy caged electron at the proton scale has not been
cleanly confirmed (a symmetric proton cage is the proper test). (iii) Reconciling the rigid-proton/
fluid-electron picture with the measured proton/electron mass ratio.

\section{Speculative outlook}\label{sec:outlook}
The same ingredients extend, far more speculatively, to a cosmological narrative --- equal-mass $\pm$ charges
read off as the curvature of two fluctuating potentials, a gravitational collapse--bounce with negative-mass
dark energy, and a primordial helium fraction $Y\approx0.245$ --- developed, with its (substantial) open
problems up front, in a companion essay~\cite{realcos}; it is mentioned here only to indicate scope and is not
part of the present, structural claim.
