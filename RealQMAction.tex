\begin{abstract}
Real Quantum Mechanics (RealQM) represents $N$ electrons by $N$ unit charge densities carried on
non-overlapping domains of ordinary three-dimensional space, the domains meeting at free boundaries whose
location is not given in advance. In the established, real-valued theory --- the one that computes atoms,
molecules and nuclei --- the ground state minimises the Coulomb energy, and on the free boundaries two
conditions are to hold: continuity of the wave function (hence of the charge density) and a vanishing normal
derivative (the homogeneous Neumann, or Bernoulli, condition). These two conditions fix the boundary and the
static problem is well posed. We observe that the \emph{time-dependent}, complex-valued extension --- used to
reach currents, magnetism and decay --- changes this. A complex wave function carries a current, and
requiring each electron to keep its unit charge as the boundary moves imposes a further condition, that the
boundary move with the flow. Continuity, Neumann, and per-electron charge conservation are then three
conditions on one free boundary: the time-dependent free-boundary problem is \emph{overdetermined}. It
cannot in general satisfy all three, and one must choose which to keep --- keep the wave function continuous
(the Bernoulli condition, with charge free to migrate between domains), or keep each electron's charge (with
a jump in the wave function). We state the conditions plainly and record this fork; its resolution is left
open. The real-valued static theory is unaffected: there the currents vanish, the boundary is at rest, and
the two Bernoulli conditions hold without conflict.
\end{abstract}

\setstretch{1.06}

\section{The real-valued theory and its free boundary}
\label{sec:static}

RealQM represents $N$ electrons by densities $\psi_i^2$, each carried on its own region
$\Omega_i\subset\mathbb R^3$, the regions non-overlapping, $\Omega_i\cap\Omega_j=\varnothing$
\cite{RealQM}. The wave functions $\psi_i$ are real, each of unit charge,
\begin{equation}
\int_{\Omega_i}\psi_i^2=1 ,
\label{eq:unit}
\end{equation}
and the ground state minimises the Coulomb energy
\begin{equation}
E=\sum_i\tfrac12\!\int_{\Omega_i}|\nabla\psi_i|^2+\tfrac12\!\iint\frac{\rho(x)\rho(y)}{|x-y|}\,dx\,dy,
\qquad \rho=\sum_i q_i\psi_i^2 ,
\label{eq:E}
\end{equation}
over the wave functions and, jointly, over the partition into domains. Minimising over the $\psi_i$ gives,
on each domain, a one-electron eigenvalue equation in the self-consistent Coulomb field --- so each domain
carries its own Poisson problem for its Hartree potential, coupled to the others only through the fields
they produce.

The interfaces $\Gamma$ between domains are not prescribed; they are located by the requirement that two
conditions hold there.
\begin{itemize}
\item[(i)] \emph{Continuity.} The total $\Psi=\sum_i\psi_i$ is continuous, so at an interface between
domains $1$ and $2$,
\begin{equation}
\psi_1=\psi_2\quad\text{on }\Gamma,\qquad\text{hence}\qquad \rho_1=\rho_2 .
\label{eq:cont}
\end{equation}
Since $\rho_i=\psi_i^2$, continuity of the wave function \emph{is} continuity of the charge density.
\item[(ii)] \emph{Vanishing normal derivative} (homogeneous Neumann, the Bernoulli condition):
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial\psi_i}{\partial n}=0\quad\text{on }\Gamma .
\label{eq:neumann}
\end{equation}
\end{itemize}
Condition (i) matches the two densities where the domains meet; condition (ii) fixes the location of the
interface. With both imposed the static free-boundary problem is well posed, and it is this problem --- unit
charges, continuity, and Neumann, coupled by Coulomb --- that reproduces the periodic table, chemical
bonding, and, read for protons and electrons, nuclear binding \cite{RealQM,RealNucleus}. Everything here is
static and real: the density is fixed in time, no charge flows across any interface, and the two conditions
\eqref{eq:cont}--\eqref{eq:neumann} sit together without tension.

\section{The time-dependent extension carries a current}
\label{sec:complex}

To reach phenomena that are absent from a static real density --- currents, magnetism, radiation, decay ---
the wave functions are made \emph{complex} and evolved by the Schr\"odinger equation generated by
\eqref{eq:E},
\begin{equation}
i\,\partial_t\psi_i=-\tfrac12\nabla^2\psi_i+q_i\,\phi[\rho]\,\psi_i ,
\label{eq:tdse}
\end{equation}
with $\phi[\rho]$ the Coulomb potential of the total density. A complex $\psi_i$ carries a current
\begin{equation}
\mathbf j_i=\operatorname{Im}(\psi_i^*\nabla\psi_i),\qquad \partial_t\rho_i+\nabla\!\cdot\mathbf j_i=0 ,
\label{eq:current}
\end{equation}
which is identically zero when $\psi_i$ is real. This current is what a static real density lacks and what
the complex extension is for.

\section{The free boundary is overdetermined in time}
\label{sec:over}

Now let the boundary move, with normal speed $V_n$. The charge of electron $i$ is
$Q_i=\int_{\Omega_i(t)}\rho_i$, and with the interface in motion, using \eqref{eq:current},
\begin{equation}
\frac{dQ_i}{dt}=\int_{\Gamma}\big(V_n\,\rho_i-\mathbf j_i\!\cdot\!\mathbf n\big)\,dS .
\label{eq:dQ}
\end{equation}
For each electron to keep its unit charge \eqref{eq:unit} as the boundary moves, the integrand must vanish,
which is a \emph{third} condition on the interface:
\begin{equation}
V_n=\frac{\mathbf j_i\!\cdot\!\mathbf n}{\rho_i}\quad\text{on }\Gamma,
\label{eq:material}
\end{equation}
the statement that the boundary moves with the charge flow. Imposed for both domains, \eqref{eq:material}
also demands $\mathbf j_1\!\cdot\!\mathbf n/\rho_1=\mathbf j_2\!\cdot\!\mathbf n/\rho_2$, a matching of the
flow speeds across the interface.

We now have \emph{three} conditions to be satisfied on one free boundary: continuity \eqref{eq:cont},
Neumann \eqref{eq:neumann}, and charge conservation \eqref{eq:material}. That is one condition too many.
The free boundary carries a single location (one function on $\Gamma$) to be fixed, and the fields already
obey their bulk equation \eqref{eq:tdse}; three interface conditions on that single freedom leave the
time-dependent free-boundary problem \emph{overdetermined}. It cannot, in general, satisfy all three at
once.

Two remarks make the tension concrete. First, \eqref{eq:neumann} and \eqref{eq:material} clash directly:
Neumann gives $\partial_n\psi_i=0$, hence $\mathbf j_i\!\cdot\!\mathbf n=\operatorname{Im}(\psi_i^*
\partial_n\psi_i)=0$, and then \eqref{eq:material} forces $V_n=0$ --- the boundary cannot move at all.
Second, continuity \eqref{eq:cont} is a condition on the \emph{value} of $\psi_i$ at the interface, whereas
charge conservation \eqref{eq:material} is a condition on its \emph{gradient} (through $\mathbf j$); the two
are independent, and holding both while the boundary moves is not automatic.

\section{The fork}
\label{sec:fork}

Faced with an overdetermined interface one must drop a condition. There are two natural choices.
\begin{itemize}
\item[(A)] \emph{Keep the wave function continuous.} Retain continuity \eqref{eq:cont} and Neumann
\eqref{eq:neumann} --- the same two conditions as the static theory --- and let the boundary sit where they
hold, moving as the fields evolve. The density stays continuous, $\Psi$ remains regular, and the energy is
conserved (with Neumann the interface contributes no energy flux and, by \eqref{eq:cont}, no net energy from
the moving boundary). What is given up is \eqref{eq:material}: as the boundary sweeps, charge migrates from
one electron's domain into the other's, so the individual $Q_i$ are not conserved (their sum is). This is
the direct continuation of the static Bernoulli conditions, and it is the choice the existing RealQM
level-set relaxation already realises.
\item[(B)] \emph{Keep each electron's charge.} Retain \eqref{eq:material}, so the boundary moves with the
flow and every $Q_i$ stays equal to one. The interface is then a contact discontinuity: the flow speeds
match across it while the density is allowed to jump, so $\psi$ (and $\rho$) is discontinuous and continuity
\eqref{eq:cont} is given up. This conserves per-electron charge at the price of a wave function that is no
longer continuous across a moving interface.
\end{itemize}
Both reduce to the static Bernoulli conditions at rest: when the currents vanish, \eqref{eq:material} reads
$V_n=0$, the boundary stops, and continuity plus Neumann hold together. The two choices differ only when the
boundary genuinely moves --- that is, only in real-time, multi-domain dynamics.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:concl}

The real-valued RealQM has a well-posed free boundary: unit charges, continuity of the wave function (hence
of the density), and a vanishing normal derivative, coupled by Coulomb. These conditions sit together
without conflict because the density is static and no charge flows. The complex, time-dependent extension
introduces a current, and with it a third interface condition --- that each electron keep its charge as the
boundary moves. Three conditions on one free boundary overdetermine it; the time-dependent free boundary
cannot satisfy continuity, Neumann, and charge conservation together, and one must choose (A) a continuous
wave function with charge free to migrate, or (B) conserved per-electron charge with a discontinuous wave
function. Which the physics of real-time dynamics selects we leave open. It is worth being clear that this
concerns only the time-dependent extension: the real-valued theory, which carries the program's established
results, is untouched --- there the currents vanish, the boundary is at rest, and the Bernoulli conditions
hold.
