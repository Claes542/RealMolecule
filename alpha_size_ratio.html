The Fine-Structure Constant as a Size Ratio

The hydrogen atom (p + e, size a0) and the deuteron (p + e + p, size Rp) in one Coulomb picture — α² = Rp/a0. Part of the RealQM/RealNucleus program.
Status — a structural/interpretive proposal. The claim is the relation α² = Rp/a0 read off the H atom and the deuteron; the underlying primitive (proton size, or the classical radius) is stated as open (§6). A cosmological extension is mentioned only as a brief outlook (§7) — see the companion Real Cosmology essay.

Abstract

The hydrogen atom is a proton with an electron shell of size a0 (the Bohr radius). The deuteron — the ²H nucleus — is, in a pure proton–electron picture (neutron = proton + electron), the system p + e + p: two protons bound by one shared electron, of size Rp (a few fm). The two are the same Coulomb physics — protons and one electron — differing only in the electron's size: bound by a single proton's charge it relaxes to a0; caged between two protons it compacts to the proton size Rp. The nuclear/atomic size ratio is then the fine-structure constant squared, α² = Rp/a0 — so α is the proton-to-atom size ratio, a pure ratio of two lengths, independent of the mass m and the speed of light c, not a coupling constant. Bracketed by two validations: atomic total energies <1% across the periodic table (a0 side) and the He-4/deuteron binding ratio 13.1 vs 12.7 (~3%, Rp side). Mass plays no role (separate, gravitational); no strong force.

1. The question

Why is the nucleus ~104–105 times smaller than the atom containing it? In standard physics the atomic scale is electromagnetic and the nuclear scale is the strong force — two unrelated sectors — and α = e²/ℏc is the electromagnetic coupling. We point out that the simplest atom and the simplest composite nucleus — the hydrogen atom and the deuteron — are, in a pure proton–electron picture, the same system at two sizes, and their size ratio is α². The argument is elementary and uses no relativity.

2. Two systems, one physics: the H atom and the deuteron

An electron is a normalised charge cloud ψ with energy E = κ∫|∇ψ|² − Z e²/L, where κ = ℏ²/2m is a kinetic coefficient. The whole structure needs only three inputs: the charge e², the coefficient κ, and the proton's finite size Rp — no mass, no strong force, no c.

Hydrogen atom: p + e, size a0. One proton, one electron. Attracted to the proton's charge, the electron must taper to zero in vacuum; that varying profile costs kinetic energy ~κ/L², and minimising κ/L² − e²/L gives L = a0 = 2κ/e² ≈ 0.53 Å — the electron shell size set by the proton's charge. The proton's finite size is irrelevant here (effectively a point).

Deuteron: p + e + p, size Rp. The deuteron (²H) is a proton plus a neutron; with neutron = p + e, it is d = p + n = p + (p+e) = p + e + p: two protons bound by one shared electron — the same constituents as the H atom plus a second proton, the nuclear analogue of the molecular ion H2+. Now the electron is caged between the protons with a multiphase free boundary: its density does not drop to zero but hands off to the proton density. A flat caged density then has ∇ψ = 0 ⇒ zero kinetic energy; no kinetic penalty for being small. Its size is set by the proton's finite size Rp (the cage floor that stops collapse), binding Coulomb (~MeV at fm) — size set by the proton's size.

Same physics, two sizes. Both are pure proton–electron Coulomb; the electron simply has no fixed size. The distinction is proton charge vs. proton size:

3. α as a size ratio

α² = Rp/a0 = (deuteron size)/(atomic size),    α = √(Rp/a0)

This is the textbook relation re/a0 = α² in disguise. The electron's three lengths — Bohr a0 = ℏ²/me², Compton λ̅C = ℏ/mc, classical re = e²/mc² — sit on the ladder a0 : λ̅C : re = 1 : α : α², so re/a0 = α². Our relation is this with the deuteron size identified as the classical radius, Rp ≈ re ≈ 2.8 fm. So α is reframed geometrically — and ``why α ≈ 1/137'' becomes ``why is the deuteron ~fm while the atom is ~Å'', relocated to the proton size (an input).

α is independent of m and c. Written as a ratio of two lengths, α = √(Rp/a0) is obtained by measuring two sizes and dividing — the deuteron and the atom. It therefore carries no mass m and no speed of light c: pure geometry, dimensionless by construction, fixed once the two lengths are given. The standard form e²/ℏc writes the same number through c (and, via a0 = ℏ²/me², through m) — but the ratio itself needs neither. In this program the primitive is the kinetic coefficient κ (so a0 = 2κ/e²), not the mass, and there is no c at all — the construction is non-relativistic and electrostatic. α is thus the most primitive reading: a bare geometric fact about how much larger the atom is than the nucleus.

4. Empirical check

The size ratio. H atom a0 = 0.53 Å ≈ 53,000 fm; deuteron rms radius ≈ 2.1 fm. Ratio d/H ≈ 1/25,000, vs α² ≈ 1/18,800 — ~30% agreement, the deuteron sitting at the classical electron radius (2.8 fm). The nuclear/atomic size ratio comes out as 1/α² from charge, ℏ and the proton size alone, no strong force. (The deuteron is loosely bound, so it is the scale/ratio being right, not a precise fit.)

The two scales are independently validated, bracketing the relation. The same Coulomb program is quantitative at each scale α² = Rp/a0 connects:

  • Atomic side (a0): RealQM reproduces atomic total energies to <1% across the whole periodic table (all Z ≥ 11; the period-2 p-block B–F the only 3–6% exception) [atoms paper].
  • Nuclear side (Rp): the same proton–electron Coulomb model gives the He-4/deuteron binding-energy ratio 13.1 vs 12.7 experimental (~3%, nested charge-cloud shells) [RealNucleus].

So α² = Rp/a0 sits between two independent quantitative validations — the atomic scale to <1% and the nuclear binding ratio to ~3% — not merely the ~30% size check.

5. Mass is a separate quantity

Nothing above used mass: the H atom and the deuteron are built from charge and κ. Mass is inertia — the localisation (frequency / inverse size) of the matter wave, the dispersion gap ω0 = mc²/ℏ — a separate, gravitational quantity (signed, hence not the positive-definite EM field energy), coupling to the electrostatic sector only through energy (E = mc²). Electromagnetism (charge) is an interaction; mass is the inverse size of each matter wave.

6. Complexity from the size asymmetry, and open problems

The asymmetry between proton and electron is not of charge (exactly balanced, ±e) but of size: the proton fixed-and-small (a rigid anchor), the electron variable. The fixed proton anchors; the variable electron spans scales — a shell in the atom, a cage-filling cloud in the deuteron — the nested hierarchy that is complexity. With fixed size for both, one scale, nothing complex. α measures this asymmetry (inverse dynamic range 1/α² ≈ 18,800): the smallness of α is the engine of complexity.

Open: (i) the nuclear primitive — proton size Rp as input length, or the relativistic classical radius (electromagnetic mass, Many-Minds Relativity); α's value is reinterpreted, not derived. (ii) Whether the solver realises a flat zero-KE caged electron at the proton scale is not cleanly confirmed (a symmetric proton cage is the proper test). (iii) Reconciling the rigid-proton/fluid-electron picture with the measured proton/electron mass ratio.
