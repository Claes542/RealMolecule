The Fine-Structure Constant as a Size Ratio

Atomic and nuclear binding in one Coulomb picture — the variable-size electron and α = √(Rp/a0). Part of the RealQM program.
Status — a structural/interpretive proposal. The claim is the relation α² = Rp/a0 and its consequences; the underlying primitive (proton size, or the relativistic classical radius) and the numerical realisation are stated as open (§7). A cosmological extension is mentioned only as a brief outlook (§8) — see the companion Real Cosmology essay.

Abstract

A single Coulomb / multiphase-continuum model (RealQM) describes both atomic and nuclear binding through a variable-size electron, using only three inputs — the charge / Coulomb potential e², an electron kinetic coefficient κ, and the proton's finite size Rp — with no mass and no strong force. The electron has no fixed size: it spreads to the atomic scale a0 = 2κ/e² when bound to one proton's charge, and compacts to the nuclear scale, set by the proton-size cage, where a flat caged density on a multiphase free boundary carries zero kinetic energy. The nuclear/atomic size ratio is then the fine-structure constant squared, α² = Rp/a0 — so α is the proton-to-atom size ratio, a geometric quantity, not a coupling constant, requiring no c. The measured deuteron/hydrogen size ratio matches 1/α² to ~30%. Mass appears nowhere in this electrostatic structure; it is a separate, inertial/gravitational quantity. Since the proton is rigid and the electron fluid, α is the electron's dynamic range across scales — and its smallness is what permits the multi-scale structure of matter.

1. The question

Why is the nucleus ~104–105 times smaller than the atom containing it? In standard physics the atomic scale is electromagnetic and the nuclear scale is the strong force — two unrelated sectors — and α = e²/ℏc is the electromagnetic coupling. We point out that in RealQM (quantum mechanics as multiphase Coulomb continuum mechanics; unit-charge densities on non-overlapping domains with Bernoulli free boundaries [1,2]) both scales arise from one mechanism with a variable-size electron, and α acquires a direct geometric meaning. The argument is elementary and uses no relativity.

2. The model and its three inputs

An electron is a normalised charge cloud ψ of size L with energy E = κ∫|∇ψ|² − Z e²/L, where κ = ℏ²/2m. The whole electrostatic structure needs just three inputs: (i) the charge e², (ii) the kinetic coefficient κ, (iii) the proton's finite size Rp. Mass does not appear (replaced by κ); neither does the strong force, nor c. Mass and gravity are a separate sector (§6).

3. The variable-size electron: two scales

Outside (atomic). Bound to one proton, the electron must taper to zero in vacuum; the varying profile costs kinetic energy ~κ/L², and minimising κ/L² − e²/L gives L = a0 = 2κ/e² — set by the proton's charge. The proton's finite size is irrelevant here (it looks like a point).

Inside (nuclear). Caged among protons with a multiphase free boundary, the electron's density does not drop to zero — it hands off to the proton density. A flat caged density then has ∇ψ = 0 throughout ⇒ zero kinetic energy; no kinetic penalty for being small. (The obstacle “confinement costs κ/L²” applies only to a vacuum electron forced to vanish at its edge.) With no kinetic pressure, the size is set by the proton's finite size (the cage floor that stops collapse), and the binding is Coulomb at that size (~MeV at fm).

So the electron is fluid (a0 outside, Rp inside) and the proton rigid (fixed size). The factor distinguishing the two scales is proton charge vs. proton size.

4. α as a size ratio

α² = Rp/a0 = (proton size)/(atomic size),    α = √(Rp/a0)

That is, α is the ratio of the proton size to the atomic size — geometric, not a coupling, and with no c. It coincides with the textbook α = e²/ℏc precisely when Rp equals the classical radius re = e²/mc² = α²a0 ≈ 2.8 fm. The electron's three lengths form the ladder a0 : λ̅C : re = 1 : α : α² (atomic, Compton, classical — the three ways to build a length from ℏ,m,c,e, each a factor α apart). The model reframes “why α ≈ 1/137” as “why is the proton ~fm while the atom is ~Å” — relocating it to the proton size (an input), interpreting α as the proton-to-atom size ratio.

So the nuclear/atomic size ratio comes out as 1/α² from charge, ℏ and the proton size alone, no strong force. (The deuteron is loosely bound, so it is the scale/ratio being right, not a precise fit.)

The two scales are independently validated, bracketing the relation. Beyond the size ratio, the same Coulomb program is quantitative at each of the two scales α² = Rp/a0 connects:

  • Atomic side (a0): RealQM reproduces atomic total energies to <1% across the whole periodic table (all Z ≥ 11; the period-2 p-block B–F the only 3–6% exception) [atoms paper] — the charge-set scale is quantitative.
  • Nuclear side (Rp): the same proton–electron Coulomb model gives the He-4/deuteron binding-energy ratio 13.1 vs 12.7 experimental (~3%, nested charge-cloud shells) [RealNucleus] — the cage-set scale is quantitative.

So α² = Rp/a0 sits between two independent quantitative validations — the atomic scale to <1% and the nuclear binding ratio to ~3% — not merely the ~30% size check.

6. Mass is a separate quantity

Nothing above used mass: the electrostatic atom and nucleus are built from charge and κ. Mass is inertia — the localisation (frequency / inverse size) of the matter wave, the dispersion gap ω0 = mc²/ℏ — a separate, gravitational quantity (signed, hence not the positive-definite EM field energy), coupling to the electrostatic sector only through energy (E = mc²). Electromagnetism (charge) is an interaction; mass is the inverse size of each matter wave — which is why the electromagnetic atom “has no room for mass.”

7. Complexity from the size asymmetry, and open problems

The structure-building asymmetry is not of charge (exactly balanced, ±e) but of size: the proton is fixed-and-small (a rigid anchor), the electron variable. The fixed proton anchors; the variable electron spans scales (atoms when spread, nuclei when caged) — the nested hierarchy that is complexity. With fixed size for both, one scale, nothing complex. α measures this asymmetry (the electron's inverse dynamic range, 1/α² ≈ 18,800): the smallness of α is the engine of complexity.

Open: (i) the nuclear primitive — proton size Rp as input length, or the relativistic classical radius (Many-Minds Relativity [3]); α's value is reinterpreted, not derived. (ii) Numerical realisation — whether the solver yields a flat zero-KE caged electron at the proton scale is not cleanly confirmed (direct runs inconclusive; a symmetric cage is the proper test). (iii) Reconciling the rigid-proton/fluid-electron picture with the measured proton/electron mass ratio.
