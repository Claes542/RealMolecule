To the Editors, International Journal of Quantum Chemistry

Dear Editors,

I submit for your consideration the manuscript "A 3D Multiphase Continuum
Computational Model for Atoms."

This submission follows a suggestion from the Editor of the Journal of
Computational Chemistry, who, on declining the manuscript as outside that
journal's scope, recommended the International Journal of Quantum Chemistry as
the more appropriate venue. I am grateful for the redirection: the paper is
indeed a contribution to quantum-chemical method and model development rather
than to applied computational chemistry, and IJQC's remit -- the methods,
models, and foundations of quantum chemistry -- is its natural home.

RealQM is a general, in-principle parameter-free, low-cost real-space method
for quantum mechanics, formulated as 3D continuum mechanics: the N-electron
state is a sum of one-electron components, each carrying unit charge on a
non-overlapping domain of physical 3D space separated by free (Bernoulli)
boundaries, with the ground state the minimizer of the standard Coulomb
energy over both the components and the domain partition. Spatial exclusion
replaces wave-function antisymmetry, so the cost scales with the number of
mesh points rather than with the dimension of an N-body configuration space,
and the energy functional contains no fitted constants.

This paper puts the method to a stringent test: it computes the total
energies and first ionization energies of all the atoms of the periodic
table. The computation is carried out in spherical symmetry, reflecting the
electronic structure of an atom, so the whole table is covered in about a
minute on a laptop. The results are: total energies within about 1% across
the periodic table (transition metals included, carried by a reference core;
the period-2 p-block and the late 3d block the only exceptions); first
ionization energies within 10-20% for the s-block and the heavier p-block;
the closed octet realized geometrically as 4+4 (two tetrahedra forming a
cube), never as a single eight-shell; and the correct ionization ordering
throughout, including 4s before 3d across the transition series. The
organizing finding is one of symmetry: the inner shells, and most valence
shells, are spherically symmetric and captured exactly; only two specific
valence geometries -- the closed octet and the interpenetrating 4s/3d valence
-- break spherical symmetry and require the full 3D solve, which sets the
quantitative limits on the light-p-block and transition-metal ionization
energies. These limits are stated plainly, not concealed: the paper reports
where the spherically reduced model reaches its accuracy and what the full 3D
valence solve is expected to recover, so that the method is presented as a
falsifiable, reproducible computational scheme rather than a finished theory.

The general formulation and its wider validation across chemistry --
molecules, covalent bonds, atomization energies, and reactions -- are given
in a companion article, "Real Quantum Mechanics" (submitted to Annales de la
Fondation Louis de Broglie), and in the accompanying monograph. The present
paper is self-contained and focused on the atoms: the periodic table as a
test of the method.

I believe the work fits IJQC as a parameter-free, real-space alternative to
basis-set and density-functional treatments for systems dominated by
electrostatic structure -- a methodological and conceptual contribution to
how the electronic structure of atoms can be computed -- presented with a
complete, reproducible implementation that runs in a web browser on a laptop.
All source code, the validation, and an interactive gallery are publicly
available at the URLs given in the manuscript, and I would encourage referees
to re-run the periodic table for themselves.

The manuscript is original, has not been published previously, and is not
under consideration elsewhere. The author has no conflicts of interest to
declare. In the interest of full transparency, the numerical implementation
was developed with the assistance of an AI coding assistant (Claude,
Anthropic); the mathematical formulation and all scientific claims are the
author's own.

Thank you for considering this submission.
