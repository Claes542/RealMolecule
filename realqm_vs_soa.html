RealQM vs the State of the Art — An Honest Assessment

What RealQM can and cannot do, and where the mainstream is more — and less — reliable than it looks. Deliberately unflattering to both sides.
Purpose. This is a candid self-assessment, not a sales pitch. Its aim is credibility: to state exactly where RealQM has something to offer, where it does not, and where the incumbent methods are oversold. Overselling is what gets a non-standard method dismissed on sight; honesty is the more defensible position.

Keep two questions apart

Most arguments about “does quantum chemistry work” conflate two very different questions. Both the mainstream and its critics stumble here.

  • (a) Does quantum chemistry have any reliable, validated methods? — a question about foundations.
  • (b) Do standard methods reliably fill the applied need (for drugs: will this molecule bind, how tightly, is it active)? — a question about practice at scale.

The answers are different: (a) yes, (b) much less than advertised.

1. What the mainstream does reliably — do not compete here

For small molecules the wavefunction hierarchy is genuinely convergent and validated:

HF → MP2 → CCSD → CCSD(T) → FCI

CCSD(T) is a real gold standard — sub-kcal/mol agreement with experiment on thermochemistry, and a systematic way to improve and estimate error. Geometries, vibrational frequencies, and qualitative electronic structure are largely solved. This is not a myth, and RealQM has nothing to add here. Any honest case for RealQM must concede this first.

2. Where the mainstream is oversold — the actual opening

The reliability collapses precisely where the validated methods do not scale — the systems that matter for applications. There you fall back on approximations whose reputation outruns their prospective performance:

  • DFT. The exchange–correlation functional is not derived; there are hundreds of functionals; results depend on the choice; dispersion is bolted on empirically; and there is no rigorous error bar and no systematic way to improve within DFT. It is an uncontrolled approximation presented as first-principles.
  • Docking / scoring. Decent at pose (where a ligand sits), poor at affinity — score-vs-experiment correlations are notoriously weak. “Docking power » scoring power” is a standing embarrassment.
  • FEP / alchemical free energy. The lead-optimisation gold standard, and it does deliver ~1 kcal/mol — but only in a narrow regime (congeneric series, no large conformational change, no protonation/tautomer shift, no metals, converged sampling). Prospective performance trails the retrospective benchmarks.
  • The scaling wall. The one method with real error bars (CCSD(T)) is exponentially expensive and never reaches a drug–protein complex. So at application scale there is no exact-but-slow option actually in use — everything running is an uncontrolled approximation.
The honest summary of applied practice: in real drug discovery, computation is triage, not prediction — used because it is cheap and it is what is available, not because it robustly works. Discovery remains overwhelmingly experimental; attrition stays brutal. The reputation of the computational stack is inflated relative to its prospective reliability. That gap is real — and it is the only honest opening for an alternative.

3. What RealQM is

RealQM recasts quantum mechanics as multiphase 3D continuum mechanics: unit-charge electron densities on non-overlapping domains separated by free boundaries, minimising the Coulomb energy directly in real space. No basis set, no exchange–correlation functional, no antisymmetry postulate (spatial exclusion replaces Pauli), and forces come directly from the charge potentials. Cost scales with grid resolution (fixed), not steeply with electron count.

Proofs of concept, all giving the right qualitative sign from first principles: omeprazole acid activation (protonation activates C2), GAD65 / PLP electron sink, β-lactam strain activation, and the nucleus/atom size picture. These are mechanistic results — how a bond forms and what activates it — not affinities.

5. What RealQM cannot do — stated first, not buried

  • No binding affinity. Reversible-ligand binding is dominated by dispersion and solvation entropy; RealQM has no dispersion and runs gas-phase. It cannot rank affinities. This is a fundamental gap, not a tuning issue.
  • No quantitative energetics. Present results are model-only (reduced valence, coarse grid, rigid geometry) — sign/trend, not barriers or ΔG.
  • Anions, heavy metals, whole proteins/folding — outside the regime.
  • Not validated. The demonstrations are exploratory, hand-built, gas-phase. There is no systematic benchmark against experiment yet. RealQM is a research program, not a finished tool.

6. The honest positioning

The incumbent being oversold does not make RealQM ready — RealQM cannot do affinity or dispersion either. “Their method is a myth” is not evidence that “ours works.” The trap catches both sides.

So the defensible move is not “we cover what they can’t.” It is: find the narrow thing the standard stack does unreliably — covalent-mechanism activation, protonation-dependent reactivity, a force-based real-space account of the reactive step, exactly where DFT’s functional-dependence and gas-phase FEP struggle — and show RealQM does that reliably and reproducibly. RealQM is then complementary to the mainstream (use CCSD(T)/FEP for energetics and affinity; RealQM for the force/mechanism picture), not a competitor on affinity.

7. Validation is a ratio, not a decimal — the honest scoreboard by scope

Validation is not “match one case to ten decimals” — that is consistent with tuning. It is untuned generality: a ratio of independent predictions to free inputs, over a stated problem scope. QED is validated not by g−2’s twelve figures but by many independent high-precision predictions from a handful of constants; a fitted functional that matches one number has ratio ~1 and validates nothing. The same yardstick, applied honestly to both methods, gives this scoreboard — and it must be read by problem scope, because “is quantum chemistry validated?” has no single answer:

Reading the scoreboard honestly: StdQM is validated narrowly and genuinely (few-body, small molecules) and weakly where it is actually used (DFT-scale, affinity) — its reputation is credibility laundered from the validated corner onto the fudged workhorse it cannot replace at scale. RealQM has only a few untuned-breadth fragments — but spread across an unusually wide scope (atoms, reactions, nuclei) from very few inputs, which is a bid for a high prediction-to-input ratio. The fragments are sparse, mostly qualitative, and not yet independently replicated. So: StdQM is far ahead in the validated corners; RealQM is a wide-but-thin sketch — both judged by the same ratio, over the same honest scope.

8. A potential user: pharma — an honest side-by-side

Take a drug company as one concrete user. Its question is narrow: does this help make a better drug faster, or cut attrition? — through binding affinity (the prize) or mechanism. Here is what each method honestly offers, and the validation each would actually put on the table.

The honest matchup. Today only StdQM touches the core pharma need — affinity — via FEP-in-regime plus institutional embedding; RealQM cannot compete there and should not pretend to. RealQM’s only honest pharma pitch is the narrow niche: a force-based mechanistic account of covalent and protonation-driven reactivity, exactly where StdQM is itself weakest (functional-dependent, few error bars) — offered as a complementary tool, contingent on a real validation (right sign and ordering across a known covalent series). The realistic ask is a pilot on one series, not adoption.

The double standard, stated plainly. Pharma would demand of RealQM exactly the prospective, benchmarked validation that StdQM is largely grandfathered past — StdQM inherited its authority (mostly from few-body QED, which does not touch affinity); RealQM must earn it from scratch. The incumbent’s applied validation is real but narrow (FEP) plus inertia; the challenger’s is a wide-but-thin sketch. Neither wears the halo honestly — one has just worn it longer.

9. The bar — the same for everyone

Prospective, validated reliability on something. That is the test the mainstream partly passes (CCSD(T) for small molecules; FEP in its narrow regime) and largely fails (drug-scale affinity). It is the test RealQM must also pass, and has not yet. The next real step is not another claim but a validation: a geometry-relaxed reactive profile, and right sign and right ordering across a series of known covalent mechanisms. Until then, the honest status is: a promising, differently-founded method with mechanistic proofs-of-concept — and everything still to prove.

Why publish an unflattering assessment? Because it is the credible one. A method that states its own limits first, concedes the incumbent’s real successes, and points only at the incumbent’s genuine weak spots is far harder to dismiss than one that claims to do everything. The honesty is the argument.
