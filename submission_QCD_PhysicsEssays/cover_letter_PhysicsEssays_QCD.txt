To the Editor
Physics Essays

Dear Editor,

I am pleased to submit, for consideration in Physics Essays, the single-author manuscript
"The Binding Energy Problem QCD Never Solved -- and a Coulomb Model That Does."

The essay makes a narrow, factual observation and draws a consequence from it. The observation is
that Quantum Chromodynamics, put forward in 1973 as the fundamental theory of the strong
interaction and with it the accepted account of why nuclei exist, has in fifty-three years not
produced a single parameter-free, first-principles prediction of a nuclear binding energy -- not
the deuteron, not the alpha particle. I am careful to state this precisely and to distinguish it
sharply from any claim that QCD is wrong. QCD's demonstrated successes lie in the perturbative,
short-distance regime of quarks and gluons, where asymptotic freedom makes it calculable. Nuclear
binding lives in the opposite, long-distance, non-perturbative regime. There the binding energies
have in practice been supplied not by QCD but by fitted effective theories, whose low-energy
constants are read off the very nuclear data they then reproduce; lattice QCD, the one route that
is QCD as such, reaches light nuclei only at unphysically large quark masses, with uncontrolled
extrapolation to the physical point on which collaborations have not converged.

The deuteron carries the argument in its purest form. With only one proton it has no Coulomb
repulsion to overcome, so its 2.224 MeV is the strong nucleon-nucleon attraction acting alone --
the simplest bound state in nuclear physics, and the first number a fundamental theory of the
strong force ought to deliver. That it has not been delivered parameter-free means the shortfall
cannot be blamed on the difficulty of the multi-proton Coulomb problem; it is present already
where no such problem exists.

Against this gap the essay sets an alternative of a wholly different character: the RealNucleus
model, in which a nucleus is protons and electrons as Coulomb charge clouds bound by the electric
force alone, with no strong and no weak force. With a single scale calibrated on the deuteron and
nothing else fitted, it reproduces the alpha binding energy (~28 MeV), the alpha-to-deuteron
binding ratio (13.1 against a measured 12.7, a scale-free prediction), and the alpha-conjugate
binding ladder up to Ca-40 at ~107%, with saturation emerging rather than imposed. The two
pictures are shown to part company already at the first nucleus: where the standard deuteron is a
proton bound to a neutron by an undelivered strong force, the RealNucleus deuteron is 2p+1e -- two
like charges held together by one shared electron, exactly as one electron binds two protons in
the molecular ion H2+.

I state plainly what is and is not claimed. The essay does not assert that QCD is falsified, nor
that its strong-coupling regime is empty; and the RealNucleus alternative is offered with its own
open problems named (the spin-statistics of the confined electron, closed-shell structure, and a
deliberate silence on the neutrino), so that it can be checked and refuted rather than merely
asserted. The single calibration scale is disclosed as a unit, not a fit: every ratio and the
shape of the binding-per-nucleon curve are predictions.

This manuscript is a companion to my "RealNucleus: Nuclear Binding as Dual Coulomb Confinement,"
currently under consideration at Physics Essays, on which it draws for the underlying computations
but from which it stands independently; the present essay is a focused argument about the
binding-energy problem itself and can be read on its own. The manuscript is original, has not been
published, and is not under consideration elsewhere; there are no conflicts of interest. In
keeping with the journal's disclosure practice, I note that this manuscript was prepared with AI
assistance (Claude, Anthropic), both for drafting and for the cited RealNucleus computations; the
argument, the physical claims, and responsibility for the content are my own, reviewed and verified
by me.

Thank you for considering the manuscript.
