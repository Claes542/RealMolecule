To the Editor
Physics Essays

Dear Editor,

I am pleased to submit, for consideration in Physics Essays, the single-author manuscript
"Magnetism in RealQM: Currents, the Nuclear Electron, and the Spin Residue."

RealQM is a reformulation of quantum mechanics as multiphase continuum mechanics in real
three-dimensional space, in which electrons are real-valued charge densities on non-overlapping
domains with free boundaries. In that form the theory is deliberately real and carries no electric
current, so magnetism is absent by construction. This paper develops magnetism as a minimal,
current-bearing extension and -- this is the point I would most like the referees to weigh --
follows it honestly to the boundary where it stops.

The manuscript reports four results and one limitation. First, a complex, unitary, time-dependent
RealQM on the same domains carries a well-defined charge current, and the Bernoulli free boundary
turns out to conserve charge per domain automatically, so nothing new must be assumed; the Madelung
hydrodynamic reading thereby becomes a physical flow in real three-dimensional space for any number
of electrons, where standard quantum mechanics confines it to configuration space. Second, a
circulating charge density carries a magnetic moment quantised by its winding number, and reacts
correctly to a magnetic field: a small solver conserves norm and reproduces the Zeeman splitting
2*muB*B to four digits, while a non-circulating density is inert -- ordinary orbital magnetism from
a charge density in real space, with no spin invoked. Third, the electron confined in a nucleus is
computed as a flat, real density, hence currentless, hence carrying no magnetic moment; nuclear
moments then come out at nuclear-magneton rather than Bohr-magneton scale, dissolving the classical
thousandfold magnetic-moment objection to nuclear electrons -- and by the same flatness that makes
the electron's mass irrelevant to nuclear binding. Fourth, a single-electron spinor structure yields
g=2: from the identity (sigma.D)^2 = D^2 - q sigma.B, verified numerically, the g=2 term emerges
rather than being inserted, and an L=0 electron splits into two levels with no middle -- Stern-Gerlach
-- non-relativistically, the factor of two being the SU(2) double cover, not a relativistic effect.

The limitation is stated as plainly as the results, and I regard it as part of the contribution. The
many-electron extension fails: a closed shell comes out paramagnetic rather than diamagnetic, and
attempting to force the spins antiparallel geometrically costs energy (a spinor domain wall), so
geometry favours parallel. Closed-shell diamagnetism, and ferromagnetism, require an exchange term
that geometric non-overlap does not supply. The precise statement the paper reaches is that RealQM's
non-overlap reproduces the spatial consequences of antisymmetry (exclusion, shells, chemistry) but
not its spin consequences (singlet pairing, exchange, collective magnetism): single-particle
magnetism is delivered, collective magnetism is not. I have tried to make the paper valuable exactly
by locating that boundary precisely rather than papering over it.

The manuscript is a companion to my other RealQM papers (in particular "RealNucleus" and the
foundational RealQM article), on which it draws but from which it stands independently. It is
original, has not been published, and is not under consideration elsewhere; there are no conflicts of
interest. In keeping with the journal's disclosure practice, I note that this manuscript was prepared
with AI assistance (Claude, Anthropic), both for drafting and for the numerical computations; the
argument, the physical claims, and responsibility for the content are my own, reviewed and verified
by me.

Thank you for considering the manuscript.

Sincerely,
