Can electricity alone hold a nucleus together?

A pure-Coulomb picture of nuclear binding — and an honest account of what it does, and does not, claim.
What this is. A general-audience walk through RealNucleus, a model in which atomic nuclei are held together by ordinary electric (Coulomb) forces between protons and electrons — no separate “strong force.” It reproduces real nuclear binding energies; it also has clear limits, stated plainly here rather than glossed over.

The puzzle every nucleus poses

A nucleus is a tiny ball of positively charged protons packed together. Like charges repel — fiercely, at these distances. So the first question of nuclear physics is simply: why doesn't the nucleus blow itself apart? The textbook answer, since the 1930s, is a new fundamental force — the strong nuclear force — that acts only at very short range and glues the protons together against their electric repulsion.

An old idea, revived

Before the neutron was discovered in 1932, physicists pictured the nucleus differently: as a mix of protons and electrons. The negatively charged electrons, sitting among the protons, would electrically screen the proton–proton repulsion and pull the whole thing together — no new force needed. This picture was dropped once the neutron arrived and the strong force took its place.

RealNucleus revisits it, with one modern twist. It treats a neutron as a proton bound to an electron, and lets protons and electrons be comparable in size (rather than the electron being ~2000× lighter, as in an atom). The result is a nucleus built from two interpenetrating clouds: electrons on the inside as the glue, protons on the outside, wrapping one another — held together by nothing but Coulomb's law.

What comes out — from electricity alone

Solved as a 3D continuum-mechanics problem (the same method used elsewhere for atoms and molecules), the pure-Coulomb model reproduces the actual numbers of nuclear physics:

  • Helium-4 (the alpha particle) binds at 29 MeV vs the measured 28.3 — 103%, with one energy scale fixed on the deuteron and nothing else adjusted.
  • Saturation — the hallmark of nuclear binding. Building up ⁸Be, ¹²C, ¹⁶O by adding one “alpha unit” at a time, the binding per nucleon stays nearly constant (7.5 → 8.2 → 8.6 MeV), at a steady ~107% of experiment — and even tracks the real rise toward the iron peak.
  • The full binding-energy curve from hydrogen to uranium, including the famous iron maximum that separates energy-releasing fusion (below it) from fission (above it), traced by a single geometric formula — the fusion-to-fission turnover emerging as a balance of packing, not an imposed threshold.
The one-line claim: the binding energies of nuclei — the numbers, the saturation, the iron peak — can be reproduced by pure Coulomb interaction between protons and electrons, with a handful of geometric parameters and no strong-force postulate.

What it does not claim — read this part

Reproducing binding energies is not the same as overturning a century of physics, and the honest scope matters more than the headline:

  • It does not disprove the strong force. The strong force explains far more than binding energies — the quark substructure of the proton (seen directly in scattering experiments), particle spectroscopy, high-energy collisions. This model touches none of that. It shows only that binding energies alone do not, by themselves, force you to introduce a new force.
  • It says nothing against neutrinos. In this picture, assembling helium from hydrogen is a rearrangement of protons and electrons that “does not call for” a neutrino — but neutrinos are really detected, in reactor, solar, and supernova experiments (multiple Nobel Prizes). The model is simply silent on the weak decays that produce them: neither needed nor denied. It computes binding energetics, not decay dynamics.
  • It is a reduced, mostly qualitative model. A few geometric parameters, agreement at the ~few-to-25% level, direct simulation so far only up to oxygen-16. Heavier nuclei and the decay dynamics are open work.

What this does not address

The flip side deserves to be spelled out, because it is large. The strong and weak forces were not built only to bind nuclei; they now account for a vast range of directly measured phenomena, and this Coulomb model speaks to almost none of it:

  • The quarks inside the proton. Scattering experiments (from the late 1960s on) see three point-like constituents inside the proton — a measurement, not a theory choice. Here the proton is a single point charge with no such parts, so the model has nothing to say about the substructure experiment plainly detects.
  • The particle zoo. Hundreds of particles — pions, kaons, and many more — organized precisely by quark content. This model has only protons and electrons.
  • High-energy collisions — jets and precision collider data, the quantitative core of the strong-force theory (QCD). Untouched.
  • The details of weak decay — the continuous energy spectrum of beta decay, the neutrinos (really detected, from reactors, the Sun, and supernovae), their oscillations, and the left/right asymmetry (parity violation) of the weak force. This model computes binding, not decay dynamics, and is silent on all of it.

So the claim is deliberately narrow: nuclear binding energies alone do not force a new force — not that the strong and weak interactions are unnecessary for the physics they were later found to describe.

There is a fair historical point in the background. The strong force was introduced (Yukawa, 1935) for exactly this problem — to hold the nucleus together — and then grew into a framework that explains far more. That breadth cuts two ways: it is the theory's great success, and also a standing reason to keep asking which parts are forced by data and which are scaffolding. This Coulomb model contributes one small data point to that question — that the original motivation, binding, can be met by geometry and electricity — while leaving the rest of the framework's substantial evidence (the quarks, the particle spectrum, the weak-decay and neutrino data above) entirely untouched. It sharpens a question; it does not close one.

Why it is still interesting

Two things. First, the same parameter-free Coulomb machinery that describes atoms, molecules, hydrogen bonds, and protein folding also accounts for the nuclear binding curve — one framework, from chemistry to the nucleus, without switching physics. Second, it revives a genuine historical question: how much of what we attribute to a separate strong force is really geometry and electricity? The model does not answer that with certainty — but it poses it concretely, with runnable numbers, rather than as philosophy.
